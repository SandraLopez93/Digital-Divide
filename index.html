The unequal digital divide in Latin America

By: Sandra Lopez

Since the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, students across the region of Latin America have lost nearly 4x more days of schooling compared to students from other regions of the world, according to a recent report by Unicef.

Not all countries within the region, however, were impacted in the same way.

Nations resorted to a variety of different learning methods. From listening along to radio transmissions and televised programming, to digital applications on computers and phones.

This report will compare the various learning methods introduced to students in Brazil and Bolivia, two countries with strong disparties in connectivity and access to learning resources.

How connected were Brazil and Bolivia throughout the COVID-19 pandemic?

Wireless Connectivity

The number of connected households in Brazil however, is not equal across the nation. The most economically marginalized communities are the least connected, such as the North and Northeast region, where 46.7% of households are without internet connection. In total, 4.1 million students are completely disconnected from wireless connection in Brazil.

Radio Schooling

Radio education is often used in tandem with other applications to access worksheets and assignments. While a household may have access to a radio, not all households have a cell phone or tablet to complete assignments.

Tele-education

While 46.1% of households in Bolivia have television access, that does not necessarily mean that students used this apparatus to facilitate their learning.

Alternative resources

Alternative learning opportunities include additional resources that school systems provided to students. This includes school connectivity, applications through digital platforms, virtual tutoring, digital learning packets, and repositories of digital content.
